A conserved fibrotic extracellular-matrix brake on remyelination: cross-lesion single-nucleus mapping of oligodendrocyte differentiation arrest in multiple sclerosis

1. Background and Hypothesis

In multiple sclerosis and related white-matter injury, remyelination often fails while oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs) persist in lesions but are held short of the mature, myelinating state. Chondroitin-sulfate- and collagen-rich lesion matrix accumulates in demyelinated tissue and inhibits OPC maturation, and enzymatic or synthetic reduction of that matrix enhances remyelination in vivo (Lau et al., 2012; Keough et al., 2016). This work tests whether a defined fibrotic-ECM signaling program, sent by stromal fibroblast-like cells through collagen, laminin and CSPG ligands and received on the oligodendrocyte lineage through integrin and proteoglycan receptors, is the transcriptional signature that distinguishes arrested from myelinating nuclei, and whether that signature is reproducible enough across independently collected MS lesions to serve as a lesion-agnostic readout of remyelination competence rather than a description of one cohort. An ECM-receptor module learned from the oligodendrocyte lineage of one MS lesion atlas is expected to separate arrested from myelinating nuclei in a second, independently generated atlas above a preset discrimination bar; a module that does not transfer indicates cohort-specific signal rather than a common brake.

2. Research Strategy

2.1 Preliminary Data and Rationale

The project builds a lineage-resolved model of oligodendrocyte differentiation arrest in human MS lesions and asks whether fibrotic-ECM receptor signaling defines it, with the aim of turning a known matrix inhibition of remyelination into a quantitative, transferable transcriptional readout of lineage arrest. Two published human snRNA-seq resources supply the evidence. The subcortical MS white-matter lesion atlas of Lerma-Martin and colleagues (Lerma-Martin et al., 2024) profiles glial, stromal and immune populations across lesion stages and resolves both the oligodendrocyte lineage and the stromal fibroblast-like ECM-sender compartment the hypothesis requires; its analysis centered lesion-rim spatial niches. The chronic active MS lesion atlas of Absinta and colleagues (GEO GSE180759; Absinta et al., 2021) centered a lymphocyte-microglia-astrocyte inflammatory axis and provides a dense oligodendrocyte-lineage receiver compartment. Reading the oligodendrocyte lineage as a graded OPC-to-myelinating trajectory and treating stromal-ECM receptor signaling as a classifier of arrest along that trajectory is the analysis these studies did not perform.

The applicant's open repair-phase stroke atlas (GEO GSE276202; Weber et al., 2025) enters as a secondary cross-species replication because it resolves an injury-associated fibroblast-like population and its ECM output. In that atlas the oligodendrocyte fraction rises from 2% of intact-cortex nuclei to 9% in the infarct core, oligodendrocyte-lineage gene-set enrichment is dominated by axon-ensheathment, ECM-organization and Wnt-regulation programs consistent with disrupted maturation, and the injury-associated cells are the leading senders of COLLAGEN and LAMININ signaling to integrin-expressing receivers, defining the sender-receiver geometry the human analysis pursues.

2.2 Specific Aims

Specific Aim 1: Pseudotime Mapping of Lineage Arrest

Aim 1 subclusters the oligodendrocyte lineage in each dataset and orders nuclei along the OPC-to-myelinating trajectory with Slingshot pseudotime and RNA-velocity support to localize where nuclei accumulate and which ECM-receptor genes mark the stalled state.

Specific Aim 2: Cross-Atlas Validation of ECM Signaling

Aim 2 quantifies fibrotic-ECM signaling onto the arrested compartment with CellChat and evaluates reproducibility in a leave-one-out design, scoring how well a module learned in one human atlas separates arrested from myelinating nuclei in the held-out human atlas by AUROC. The bar is 0.75, a value at which cross-dataset cell-signature transfer is treated as reproducible rather than strong, marking a module that carries most of the arrest signal while tolerating cohort and platform differences; significance is judged against an empirical null of label-permuted modules and matched-size random gene sets, and technical rather than biological transfer is controlled by harmonizing the atlases on one-to-one shared genes with Harmony batch correction and by confirming the module does not track dataset identity in the permuted null. A failed human-to-human transfer refutes the shared-program claim. The murine stroke atlas is a secondary, cross-species check via one-to-one orthologs, where a positive result argues for a conserved matrix-to-lineage program and a negative one is uninformative about the human question given the different species and injury.

Specific Aim 3: In-Silico Ranking of Candidate Receptors

Aim 3 uses CellOracle regulatory in-silico perturbation to test whether removing the top conserved ECM receptors moves nuclei toward the myelinating branch, ranking one receptor for experimental follow-up; CellOracle is run following its published brain-development reference workflow and must first recover the intact OPC-to-oligodendrocyte transition, ordering held-out lineage cells with a rank correlation of at least 0.6 to the Slingshot pseudotime, otherwise ranking reverts to differential ligand-receptor strength along pseudotime. The deliverable is a ranked, annotated receptor and reproducible code; its experimental test in a human iPSC-derived oligodendrocyte differentiation system is a defined next step to be pursued with a glial-biology collaborator beyond this budget.

3. Investigator and Team Qualifications

Ruslan Rust is an early-career investigator whose group generated the repair-phase stroke atlas used here as the cross-disease replication and released it as open data with an interactive browser, so the applicant controls that dataset and its annotations. The atlas study applied the single-nucleus clustering, gene-set enrichment and CellChat cell-cell communication analysis this project extends, and it defined the stromal fibroblast-like population and its collagen and laminin signaling that motivate the hypothesis (Weber et al., 2025). The group's single-nucleus study of neural graft-host crosstalk resolved neurexin, neuregulin and SLIT signaling between grafted and host cells with the same ligand-receptor framework, showing the applicant applies these tools across independent datasets and cell types (Weber et al., 2025, Nature Communications). The applicant's review of the blood-brain barrier across ageing, neurodegeneration and injury supplies the vascular-stromal and delivery context in which an ECM-directed remyelination target would be evaluated (Rust et al., 2025). Harmonizing external human MS atlases and running CellOracle are additions to the group's published toolset; both extend its established snRNA-seq and CellChat workflow, CellOracle is run against its published reference pipeline, and the leave-one-out transfer test and the pseudotime-recovery benchmark in Aims 2 and 3 gate each added step before its output is used.

4. Budget and Timeline

4.1 Budget Justification

The work reanalyzes three existing open snRNA-seq resources and is matched to the $5,000 award. Compute and storage total about $4,200, covering reprocessing and integration of roughly 240,000 nuclei across the three datasets on high-memory cloud instances together with object storage and egress for the intermediate objects; about $500 covers a public data-and-code archive with a persistent identifier; and about $300 is held for figures and documentation. Preprint posting and the code repository carry no charge.

4.2 Timeline and Milestones

The plan runs six months. Months 1-2: preregister the analysis on ResearchHub, reprocess the two human MS atlases and the stroke atlas, and deliver oligodendrocyte-lineage subclustering and pseudotime maps for each (Aim 1 milestone). Months 3-4: run CellChat inference and the leave-one-out transfer test with its permutation null, reporting the AUROC against the 0.75 bar (Aim 2 milestone). Months 5-6: benchmark and run the CellOracle in-silico knockdown, rank the conserved receptor target, and release the code repository, processed objects and a preprint (Aim 3 milestone). Progress will be posted monthly on ResearchHub and all processed data and analysis code shared under an open license.
