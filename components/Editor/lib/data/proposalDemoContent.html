Investigator: Hao Yin

Supervisor: J. Geoffrey Pickering

Institution: Western University

1. Overview

1.1. Scientific rationale and importance:

Lower extremity peripheral artery disease (PAD), due to atherosclerotic narrowing or thromboembolic blockage in the arteries, affects over 200 million people worldwide1.  Its severe form, chronic limb-threatening ischemia (CLTI), leads to impaired muscle functions, non-healing ulcers, amputation, and even death.  Following surgical or endovascular revascularization, successful recovery requires coordinated angiogenesis and myogenesis in affected limbs, which is not sufficiently supported by current medical management.  Microvascular mural cells – arteriolar smooth muscle cells (SMCs) and pericytes – are essential to integrate the hypoxic, inflammatory, and mechanical inputs, whereby mediating communications with endothelial cells and myogenic progenitors in tissue injury and regeneration2.

Here, we propose to investigate a novel mechanism by which the longevity-associated protein Sirtuin 6 (Sirt6) in microvascular mural cells orchestrates ischemic muscle regeneration.  Our central hypothesis is that loss of Sirt6 in mural cells triggers an anti-regenerative state in ischemic limbs by inducing senescence-associated toxic secretome, which propagates cellular senescence, fibrosis, and inflammation in skeletal muscle interstitial space.  We further hypothesize that Sirt6 deficiency-induced paracrine senescence is orchestrated by the release of extracellular histones as components of senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs). We have obtained strong preliminary data from an inducible, Myh11+ mural cell-specific Sirt6 knockout mouse model (see Pilot Data for details). Upon hindlimb ischemia induced by femoral artery resection, these mice exhibit profound senescence in mural cells, as well as endothelial cells and interstitial fibroblast-like cells. Moreover, Sirt6-null mouse arterial SMCs exhibit increased histone transcripts in vitro and increased extra-nuclear histones in vivo. We will integrate bulk RNA-seq, cytokine array, and advanced 3D microfluidic co-culture systems (AIM Biochip) to study the secretome and cell-cell communications between mural cells and other non-myogenic cells isolated from ischemic mouse muscle, followed by integrative analysis with public single-cell transcriptome datasets. Successful completion of this project will uncover novel mechanisms of tissue failure in CLTI. Note that this project is part of an ongoing multi-year project in the Pickering lab. The anticipated findings will be combined with (i) pilot and new mouse data, and (ii) new human data (see Future Plan for details), which potentially identify Sirt6 and/or extracellular histones as promising therapeutic targets to promote the regeneration of vasculature and musculature.

2. Introduction

2.1. Background

The failure of tissue regeneration in PAD or CLTI, even after revascularization, is a major unmet clinical need. While many preclinical studies present strategies to stimulate angiogenesis, few exhibit translational benefits3. While endothelial cell (dys)functions are well recognized in ischemic limb regeneration, the role of microvascular mural cells — pericytes and arteriolar smooth muscle cells (SMCs), which stabilize blood vessels and regulate blood flow — is investigated to a rather limited extent4. Notably, recent single-cell/-nucleus transcriptomics studies provide computational evidence for mural cell communications with myogenic and non-myogenic cells in ischemic skeletal muscle5,6.

Sirtuin 6 (SIRT6) is a class III NAD+-dependent histone deacetylase that is critical to genomic stability, metabolic homeostasis, and healthy aging7. SIRT6 is reported to protect against fibrosis and inflammation in a wide variety of pathogenic scenarios, but its specific role in human PAD or mouse limb ischemia remains unknown. Our pilot data show that adult mice with induced mural cell Sirt6 KO exhibited insufficient recovery of blood flow, impaired angiogenesis, and enhanced fibrosis in the ischemic hindlimb. Our proposed work will address this knowledge gap, potentially offering a translatable avenue for promoting tissue recovery in PAD or CLTI.

Cellular senescence, a state of irreversible growth arrest featuring a pro-inflammatory senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP), is associated with the pathogenesis of age-related vascular diseases, including PAD8,9.  In SMCs and mesenchymal lineage cells, Sirt6 is a well-documented anti-senescent protein, and has been reported as a negative modulator of pro-inflammatory or pro-fibrotic transcriptional program, including NF-κB or TGFβ-SMAD signaling10,11.  Concordantly, we observed that Sirt6-deficient microvascular mural cells were senescent and defective in wrapping the regenerating capillaries and arterioles in the mouse ischemic hindlimb (see Pilot Data for details).

Recently, chromatin components, including genomic DNA and histones, have been identified as part of the senescence-associated secretome12.  Cellular uptake of such components, including histones, triggers senescence via the cGAS-STING pathway13,14.  Moreover, extracellular histones can act as a damage-associated molecular pattern (DAMP) signal to evoke sterile inflammation by activating Toll-like receptors (TLR) 2 and 415. Importantly, our pilot data show that Sirt6-null SMCs exhibited increased multiple histone transcripts in culture and elevated cytoplasmic histone content in aortic media of angiotensin II-stressed mice, without significantly exacerbated cell death, suggesting that Sirt6 may restrict histone mislocalization or release in mural cells. These lines of evidence raise an intriguing Research Question: Can histones act as a secreted mediator for Sirt6 deficiency-induced paracrine senescence, and/or anti-regenerative signaling in ischemic skeletal muscle?

2.2. Hypotheses

H1. Sirt6 deficiency in microvascular mural cells causes cell-intrinsic senescence and a pro-senescent secretome that induces senescence of neighboring endothelial cells and FAPs.

H2. Extracellular histones released by Sirt6-null microvascular function as SASP/DAMP components to amplify senescence, impair angiogenesis, and evoke fibrosis through activating the cGAS-STING pathway.

 

2.3. Impact Statement

Innovation: This proposal is highly innovative in several respects: (i) It is the first study to define the cell-autonomous and paracrine roles of SIRT6 in the context of limb ischemia.  (ii) Cellular senescence is a previously under-characterized feature in limb ischemia.  (iii) Extracellular histones act as key paracrine effectors of Sirt6 deficiency, and this link has not been reported.  (iv) The role of cGAS-STING has not been systematically investigated in limb ischemia. We will combine a temporally-controlled, cell-specific knockout mouse model (Myh11-CreERT2;Sirt6flox/flox) and a 3D microfluidic “microvessel-on-a-chip” platform (AIM Biotech idenTx Chip, see Methods for details) to interrogate the possibilities above.

Impact: The current clinical trial landscape reveals a critical translational gap in medical management for PAD.  No trials registered on ClinicalTrial.gov directly target SIRT6, histones, or cGAS-STING for PAD/CLTI.  It should be noted that extracellular histones and cGAS-STING are the active trial targets for other diseases, including cancers, autoimmune diseases, and COVID pneumonia.  Additionally, Sirt6 mRNA therapy (SirTLab) is being actively developed for improving human healthspan.  Our findings will provide a strong rationale for repurposing or designing drugs targeting these pathways for PAD.

3. Methods

This project is organized into 3 specific aims to be completed over a 6-month period. As this project is proposed as an integral part of an ongoing large project, the proposed work aims to generate proof-of-principle, instead of comprehensive, cross-supportive evidence. Furthermore, a brief statement on how the current data empowers an expanded, future research plan is included at the end of this section.

Aim 1: Characterize the senescent phenotype, secretome, and extracellular histone release of Sirt6-deficient microvascular mural cells

Working hypothesis: Sirt6 deficiency induces a robust senescent phenotype and a unique SASP, including extracellular histones, in microvascular mural cells.

Sample: Tamoxifen-induced adult Myh11-CreERT2;Sirt6flox/flox (Sirt6-SMKO) mice and tamoxifen-treated Cre-negative littermate controls. N=5 mice per genotype.

·         Microvascular Mural Cell Isolation: Hindlimb ischemia in mice will be induced by femoral artery resection. Pericytes and peri-arteriolar mural cells will be isolated from the ischemic hindlimb calf skeletal muscle (gastrocnemius, tibialis anterior, and soleus). Our team has an established MACS protocol based on enzymatic digestion and sorting for PDGFRβ+ cells16. The cell identity of isolated cells will be validated via immunostaining for positivity of PDGFRβ, NG2, and negativity of CD31, CD45, and PDGFRα. If needed, PDGFRα+ cells, representing fibroblast lineage or FAPs, can be further sorted.

·         Senescence Characterization: Primary cultured mural cells will be assessed for senescence markers: Senescence-Associated β -Galactosidase (SA-β-gal) staining, and immunofluorescent detection of cell cycle checkpoint inhibitors (p16Ink4a, p21Cip1), DNA damage (γH2AX), Lamin B1, and pan-histone. Subcellular localization (nuclei versus cytoplasm versus plasma membrane) of histones will be quantitatively analyzed via high-resolution confocal microscopy.

·         Secretome Analysis: Conditioned media from Sirt6-SMKO vs. control cells will be analyzed. A Luminex multiplex assay (RayBiotech) will quantify canonical SASP factors (e.g., IL-1α, IL-6, CCL2, TNF, MMP3). ELISA kits will be used to measure specific histones secreted (H2A, H2B, H3, H4). As histones or cytokines can be leaked from dead/dying cells, the extent of cell death will be monitored via lactate dehydrogenase assay of conditioned media, and annexin IV-propidium iodide assay of cultured cells.

·         Paracrine Senescence Assay:  Conditioned media from Sirt6-SMKO or control mural cells will be transferred to (1) control mural cells, (2) C57BL/6N mouse primary skeletal muscle mouse endothelial cells (clonally expanded from Cell Biologics, catalog# C57-6220), or (3) C57BL/6N mouse primary skeletal muscle mouse fibroblasts (Pickering lab stock). Recipient cells will be assayed for induced senescence (SA-β-gal, p16, and p21).

·         Mechanistic Roles of Extracellular Histones in Paracrine Senescence: Prior to transfer, conditioned media will be depleted of histones via incubating with a cocktail of histone-neutralizing antibodies (H3 + H2A/H4) or defibrotide, an FDA-approved polyanionic oligonucleotide mix that neutralizes histones17. Controls will be isotype-matched IgG or vehicle.  The evaluation of the efficacy of defibrotide, though not a histone-specific agent, could provide drug-repurposing potential.

Histone transfer to recipient cells (surface or cytoplasm) will be detected via immunofluorescence.

Histone-associated chromatin DNA may also trigger paracrine senescence. Its involvement will be interrogated by DNase I treatment of conditioned media.

·         cGAS-STING axis in Paracrine Senescence: Prior to condition media incubation, recipient cells will be treated with cGAS inhibitor (RU.521), STING inhibitor (C-176), or their siRNAs. The evaluation of the effectiveness of the cCAS-STING cascade offers additional drug-repurposing potential for PAD.

·         Rescue Experiment: Sirt6 dependence of key findings (e.g., representative senescence marker and released histones) will be validated via adenoviral Sirt6 overexpression in Sirt6-null cells.

Sample size and Power analysis: Based on our established protocol, 10~20k PDGFRβ+ microvascular mural cells can be isolated from each mouse. These cells can be cultured in gelatin-coated 24-well plate to confluence (~50k cells per well) and amplified for another passage at a 1:2 split ratio, without attrition of pericytic identity.  The cell experiments above will be conducted in 24-well plates. Approximately 10k cells per well are needed for immunofluorescence, and 50k cells per well for conditioned medium analysis. Based on our previous publication18 and pilot data, n=5 independent replicates can provide sufficient power to detect 30% difference in SA-β-gal activity with α = 0.05, power = 0.80.

 

Planned Analyses:

Primary analyses: Senescence readouts (SA-β-gal, p16, and p21) will be compared between two groups (Sirt6 null versus control; with versus without Sirt6 rescue; with versus without histone neutralization or cGAS-STING inhibitor) using Student t-test or Mann-Whitney U test, depending on data distribution. Secretome analysis will be subjected to multiple test correction using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure.

Secondary analyses: Histone subcellular localizations (nuclei versus plasma membrane versus cytoplasm in donor mural cells; plasma membrane versus cytoplasm in recipient cells) will be compared among different conditions using two-way ANOVA, followed by post hoc Sidak’s correction.

 

Aim 2: Model Sirt6 deficiency in paracrine senescence, angiogenesis, and fibrogenesis using a 3D microfluidic hydrogel platform

Research goal: We aim to segregate the direct impact (via secretome, extracellular matrix deposition, or physical contact) of Sirt6-null microvascular mural cells on neighboring endothelial cells or fibroblasts/FAPs from tissue-level perturbation, such as hypoxia or inflammation.  We will use the AIM Biotech idenTx Chip, a validated “microvessel-on-a-chip” microfluidic system19, to recapitulate the complex 3D, multi-cell type interactions.

Sample: Sirt6-SMKO and control mice for PDGFRβ+ microvascular mural cell isolation as Aim 1. N=6 mice per genotype.

3D Co-culture System: Mouse skeletal muscle endothelial cells will be seeded in the central channel of fibrin hydrogel, co-seeded with Sirt6-null or control mouse microvascular mural cells at a 5:1 endothelial-to-mural cell ratio. The system will be perfused with media containing VEGF-A165 to promote microvascular network formation over 7 days. This culture condition has been validated for human endothelial cell-SMC co-culture, and may require further optimization.

·         Angiogenesis Metrics: After 7 days, we will quantify CD31+ vascular network formation using confocal microscopy and image analysis. Metrics include volume-normalized vessel length, density of junctions (branching), lumen size, network connectivity, vessel permeability (assessed by leakage of 40 kDa FITC-dextran), basement membrane deposition, and SM α-actin+ mural cell investment.

·         Fibrogenesis Co-culture: In a parallel setup, mouse skeletal muscle fibroblasts, prelabeled using CellTracker deep red, will be incorporated at a 5:1 endothelial cell to fibroblast ratio20 into the hydrogel alongside ECs and mural cells (Sirt6-null or control) to assess fibrotic phenotype (immunofluorescence for collagen I, SM α-actin, LOX). Peri-cellular collagen organization can be further studied using second harmonic generation and CellTracker Deep Red imaging under two-photon microscopy (Hor, Bio-protocol. 2016 https://bio-protocol.org/en/bpdetail?id=1938).

·         Paracrine Senescence in 3D Niche: Senescence will be detected via p16 and p21 immunofluorescence in mural cells and endothelial cells in a two-cell co-culture hydrogel. In addition to the density of senescent cells, the distance between senescent mural cells and the nearest senescent versus non-senescent endothelial cells in the 3D space will be measured.

·         Extracellular histone in extracellular matrix: Immunofluorescence detection of histones in paraformaldehyde-fixed hydrogel will be first attempted. The cell body will be delineated using F-actin staining. If immunofluorescent sensitivity is too low, chip effluent will be collected, concentrated, and subjected to ELISA detection for histones.

 

Sample size and Power analysis: Each central channel of the chip requires 60~100k endothelial and 10~20k mural cells with or without fibroblasts to achieve a robust vasculogenic network. Six mice are expected to yield 300~600k microvascular mural cells in culture for 30 channels. Based on our experience in human endothelial cell-SMC coculture with a variety of pharmacological interventions and previous publications using similar self-assembling microvessel-on-chip19,20, N=5~7 is sufficient to detect 30% variation in volume-normalized vessel length with α = 0.05, power = 0.80.

 

Planned Analyses:

Primary analyses: Angiogenesis or fibrogenesis metrics and extracellular histone abundance will be compared between two groups (Sirt6 null versus control) using Student t-test or Mann-Whitney U test, depending on data distribution.

Secondary analyses: To assess the paracrine proximity of cellular senescence, we will construct a mixed-effects logistic regression model at the single-cell level.  Outcome is binary endothelial cell senescence (1/0; defined based on p16 or p21 immunofluorescent density threshold).  Predictors (fixed effects) are (i) distance to the nearest senescent mural cells (binned at 10 μm, as distance effects are usually non-linear), (ii) Sirt6 phenotype of mural cells (1, 0), and (iii) a genotype x distance interaction term.  Random effects are independent experiment replicates.  To account for the confounding by local endothelial cell density, a density term (distance to the nearest endothelial cells) will be included as a covariate.  If <5% endothelial cells are senescent when co-cultured with control mural cells, terms (ii) and (iii) will not be included in the model.

Aim 3: Bulk transcriptomics of Sirt6-deficient microvascular mural cells and integrative analysis with public single-cell/single-nucleus RNA-seq datasets

Research goal: We will perform bulk RNA-seq of freshly sorted Sirt6-null and control microvascular mural cells from ischemic and contralateral mouse calf muscle. This bulk RNA-seq dataset and public single-cell or single-nucleus RNA-seq datasets will be re-analyzed to determine (i) whether the histone transcripts and senescence transcript features are over-presented in one or more cell types in ischemic muscle specimens, and (ii) if transcript correlations between Sirt6 and histones, senescence features, or cGAS-STING features are observed in microvascular mural cells or other cell types in ischemic muscle specimens

Sample: Sirt6-SMKO and control mice for PDGFRβ+ microvascular mural cell isolation as Aim 1. N=4 mice per genotype. Published single-cell/-nucleus RNA-seq datasets (GSE233882, GSE235143)5,6 of human calf muscle from PAD subjects will be retrieved from the GEO database, including 24 PAD patients and 16 controls in total.

Bulk RNA-seq: rRNA-depleted, total RNA libraries will be constructed from freshly sorted Sirt6-null and control microvascular mural cells to retain non-polyadenylated histone mRNAs. Conventional, unbiased differential expressed genes and pathway analyses will be performed. Batch effects, if any, will be controlled using the sva R package. Primary comparisons will be between Sirt6 KO and control. Secondary comparisons will be between ischemic and contralateral. Targeted analyses include specific histone transcripts. Note that our lab has already completed a bulk RNA-seq experiment on primarily cultured Sirt6-null aortic SMCs, which exhibited elevated histone transcripts (see Pilot Data for details), providing feasibility for the proposed experiments.

Integrative RNA-seq re-analysis: As GSE235143 retains individual subject-level information but has moderate N=4 per group, and GSE233882 has N=20 and 12 but pools nuclei from multiple patients, the main analyses will be conducted using GSE235143 and GSE233882 in parallel, instead of performing a meta-analysis.

·         Histone gene module score: Three module scores will be defined based on gene sets of (i) canonical histones, (ii) H2A variants21, and (iii) histones plus histone chaperones22. Histone scores will be computed for each annotated cell type with pseudo-bulk counts and compared between PAD and control conditions.  Moreover, as histones often track cell cycle activity, the histone module score will be adjusted for the cell cycle module score.

·         Sirt6-histone association: For each cell type and PAD/control condition, Spearman correlation between Sirt6 counts and each of the 3 histone module scores will be computed.  Again, cell cycle activity will be adjusted via regression with the cell cycle module score.

·         Sirt6-senescence association: Cell type-specific senescence module scores will be computed based on SenCID23, with SID2 for pericyte/SMCs, SID4 for endothelial cells, and SID5 for fibroblasts/FAPs. Correlation analyses will be conducted as above.

·         Sirt6-cGAS-STING association: Two cGAS-STING activation module scores will be computed based on (i) Reactome gene set R-HSA-1834941 “Sting-mediated induction of host immune response”, which contains 16 genes covering documented signaling mediators, or (ii) a recently published cGAS-STING pathway score24, which contains 9 genes covering major signaling nodules and output cytokines.  Correlation analyses will be conducted as above.

 

Expanded future research plan

This proposed work is expected to yield data that is to be incorporated into the multi-year ongoing project on the biological roles of Sirt6 in limb ischemia.  The proof-of-principle data generated will be supplemented or confirmed by alternative methods that require more materials or time.  For example, protein data based on immunofluorescence will be confirmed using more cells and Western blots.  As Sirt6 functions as a transcriptional regulator, DNA repair enzyme, and genomic stabilizer, as well as a metabolic modulator, the molecular mechanisms of senescence and histone leakage induced by Sirt6 deficiency will be dissected.  Moreover, if the roles of extracellular histones and the cGAS-STING pathway are supported ex vivo, their involvement will be further investigated in the mouse model and validated in human ischemic skeletal muscle tissues archived in Pickering lab25.  Even though the proposed role of extracellular histones was disapproved by the experimental evidence, the data derived from the secretome, 3D microfluidic system, and bulk RNA-seq would help generate new hypotheses.

Ethics approvals for the use of animal tissue, cells, and adenovirus are ready in place.  All analysis R/Python codes and raw/processed sequencing datasets will be shared in a public repository (GitHub, GEO or SRA).
